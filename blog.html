For Wounds of the Heart: Why I Wrote This Book

I started writing Sorrowroot after I lost my father. It was sudden — the kind of loss that doesn't knock before it enters — and he wasn't only my father. He was my best friend. The person whose voice I checked everything against. And then one ordinary day, the voice was gone and the checking wasn't.

Except that isn't quite true, and the book is me learning why. His words didn't leave when he did. They still guide me — in decisions, in small hours, in the way I measure what matters. So I wrote a girl who grew up in a garden with a father who taught her the names and uses of things. Silverleaf for wounds. Moonpetal for fever. Sorrowroot for pain that lives in the heart. And I wrote what happens to her when he's taken from her without warning — and how everything he planted in her keeps growing anyway, keeps speaking, keeps showing her the way through.

People sometimes ask why the flower for heart-wounds is the one the book is named after, and the honest answer is that I didn't choose it any more than I chose the grief. Sorrow has roots. It goes down into the dark and stays. But I've come to believe the dark is not the end of the story — that's where the growing starts. The valley in Scripture isn't a place you're left; it's a place you're walked through. And the flowers that grow there only grow there.

So this book is for him — my first reader in everything, who never got to read it. And it's for anyone carrying a voice they can no longer call. Keep listening. What they planted in you is still growing.

The Sower and the Windsown

People ask where the trilogy's name comes from, and the truest answer is a parable. In Matthew 13, Jesus tells of a sower who went out to sow — and what strikes me every time is how recklessly he scatters. Seed on the path, seed on the rocks, seed among thorns, seed on good soil. He doesn't audition the ground first. He throws grace everywhere and lets the soil decide what it will do with it.

That generosity became the spine of these books. In my story, the wind carries what the empire tried to bury — whispers, seeds, words, a boy's prayer, a flower's pollen — and drops them in the least promising places. A sealed garden. A hardened soldier. Soil no one would have bet on. Windsown means exactly that: nothing you were given landed on you by accident, even when the ground you were standing on felt like rock.

And the parable is honest about the thorns and the scorching sun — that's what I love about it. It never pretends every seed makes it. Some of my characters are the hard path. Some are choked by what the world told them to want. The question the whole trilogy asks is the question the parable asks: what kind of ground will you be, when the seed lands on you? Because it will land. The Sower is not stingy, and the wind knows the way.

Buried Books and Whispered Truths

In 1945, near a town in Egypt called Nag Hammadi, a farmer digging for fertilizer struck a sealed jar with thirteen ancient books inside — texts buried sixteen centuries earlier, most likely to keep them from being destroyed. Among them was the Apocryphon of John: the "secret book," a revelation the apostle receives when the risen Christ appears to him in grief and doubt. Other texts from those early centuries tell of Mary Magdalene, the disciple who stayed, quietly relaying teachings entrusted to her alone — and being doubted for it, because who would hand the deepest truth to someone the world counted least?

I'm a storyteller, not a theologian, and Sorrowroot is a fantasy, not a doctrine. But those images went into me like seeds and would not stop growing: truth buried in the ground to outlast an empire. A book that survives because someone loved it enough to hide it. A whisper handed to the overlooked. If you've read my synopsis, you can see the fingerprints everywhere — a Lost Book an empire buried generations ago, and a sheltered girl no one counted who carries the whisper anyway.

What moves me most isn't the secrecy — it's the patience. A jar can wait in the dark for sixteen hundred years, and the words inside lose nothing. My characters live in a world where remembering has been made a crime, and still the truth just... waits. Under the sand. Between pressed pages. In the heart, which — as Livia's father taught her — cannot be deceived. Empires spend everything making people forget. The ground remembers for free.

On Naming a Flower That Doesn't Exist (Yet)

Every book has a word that arrives before the rest of the story does. For me, it was sorrowroot — a name I had before I knew what the flower looked like, what it meant, or who would be holding it.

I knew two things going in: it needed to be small and unremarkable enough that a girl who'd grown up surrounded by rarer, showier blooms would still call it her favorite. And it needed a use — "for wounds of the heart" — specific enough to mean something the first time it's said, and devastating the second.

The color came last. Pale blue, the kind that shows up in a clear sky or a robin's egg, with petals creased like the pages of a book left open too long. Once I had that image, the rest of the symbol built itself: a flower that looks delicate and is anything but, handed between two people who have every reason not to trust each other.

That's usually how it goes for me. The name shows up first. The meaning catches up once I've spent enough time sitting with it.

Building Sopatra: A World That Forgets

Sopatra needed to feel like paradise before it could feel like a cage. That ordering mattered more than almost anything else in the early drafts — if the reader doesn't fall in love with the place first, the moment it turns on its own people doesn't land the way it should.

So I started with what Livia would actually love about it: a library with a stained-glass dome, a garden that responds to her, animals that follow her into bed at night without being called. Soft things. Safe things. The kind of world a protective father builds when he's trying to keep his daughter from ever needing to be brave.

The wrongness had to live underneath all of that, not instead of it. Paradise as a design choice, not an accident — a place built to keep someone from remembering who she really is, dressed up in everything she'd ever want. I didn't want readers to distrust Sopatra. I wanted them to love it exactly as much as Livia does, right up until they understand why that love was never entirely hers to give.

Why I Write Villains You Fall For

Varick doesn't walk into this story as a monster. The prologue finds him a conflicted warrior just home from a mission that went awry, quietly protective of the vendor boys in the streets, listening to a wind that has always seemed to know things he doesn't. I wanted readers to meet the man before the weapon — because when he finally does the unforgivable thing, it should break something in them, too. His whisper to Livia is "Forgive me." He doesn't get to decide whether she does.

I think the difference between a redemption arc and a free pass comes down to whether the character is allowed to want forgiveness or has to earn it. Varick doesn't get to skip the cost of what he's done just because he starts caring about the person he did it to. He has to sit in that. So does the reader.

That tension — menace that's real, tenderness that's also real, and neither one canceling the other out — is the only kind of villain I'm interested in writing anymore. The scary part isn't that he might hurt her. It's that he might actually deserve her, and what that would cost him to believe. And underneath all of it, the wind keeps whispering. It knows the way — it's sown into the trilogy's name.
