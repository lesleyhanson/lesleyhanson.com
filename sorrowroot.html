The Story

Livia has never left Sopatra — a paradise of gardens and ancient libraries, sealed away by a father who loved her too carefully to let her see the world beyond it. She believes, the way she believes anything from her father's books, that the heart cannot be deceived.

Then Commander Varick walks into her home and kills her father in front of her.

Sopatra collapses into something far less forgiving, stranding Varick's Guard — and their prisoner — in a jungle that answers to no one. Livia carries a spark of something ancient: an instinctive bond with every living thing on the planet. And when it counts, she uses it to save the very man sent to destroy her life.

Varick tells himself every small kindness is just keeping the asset alive. He is bound by an oath that costs him everything the moment he lets himself feel anything else. But Livia's instincts keep pulling her toward the one person she swore to hate.

Some books were never meant to be found. Some truths were buried for a reason. And some hearts don't ask permission before they choose.

An Excerpt

Prologue: Whispers

The wind on Maitreya knew things. Varick had understood that since he was a child. He’d learned to trust it the way some men trusted maps.

It thrashed when he cut down weaker soldiers, the hot air gritty with arena dust. It whipped on the rare occasion he fought someone who could survive a few moments against him—although never more than seven swings. Sometimes, it seemed to move with intention; guiding his weapon in battle or assisting with calculations.

Tonight, it whispered.

Just like his soldiers outside.

He filed it and continued unpacking. He could hear everything they said—a gift he never telegraphed but used accordingly. He’d learned that from an early age, too.

“Something happened out there,” a woman said, her voice carrying the certainty of someone who had already done the math and didn’t like the answer. Cigarette smoke drifted past the window from a soldier who said nothing. Which meant he agreed.

Varick kept his eyes forward and squeezed the handle of his blade.

“Ah, now.” Bertram’s voice moved between them like a man who had made peace with impossible things. “He’s back. That’s what matters.”

“The question is whether coming back is better than the alternative,” the woman said.

The group went quiet, like something true had been said that no one intended to answer.

“Tomorrow’s another day,” Bertram offered. “That’s enough.” Then, softer, the words the old man always left them with: “Through the valley.”

The voices outside repeated it—a secret affirmation between those who lived it.

Varick said nothing. But tomorrow’s challengers would be dead by midday. They always were.

He weighed everything the day had given him: follow-up reports, briefings, endless orders and objectives.

Nothing that mattered.

The wind knocked against the glass along with two child-size fists. Trevor and Enzo rounded the corner and charged past the soldiers. The vendor boys arrived as they always did: barreling into spaces before anyone could redirect them.

They peeked at his face before speaking—an easy read of the evening’s mood.

“You’re back,” Enzo said. Not a question. A confirmation he’d needed.

Varick nodded without turning around.

Trevor stood quietly, calculating variables of his own. “It’s different when you’re not around.” He said it flatly, the way kids do when they’ve stopped expecting comfort. Enzo stood beside him and said nothing. Which meant everything.

Varick set his blade down and turned to face them.

“Ready for tomorrow?” Trevor’s question carried the weight of things none of them could say aloud.

Varick handed each boy a piece of shagbark. Enzo’s eyes sparkled. Trevor glanced at the hickory stick in his hand before studying Varick’s face for something more convincing. Something that would hold.

Varick held his gaze but gave him nothing.

“We don’t like it when you’re gone.” Enzo chewed the resin anxiously.

“I’m never gone for long,” Varick said finally. “Just look out for each other. And don’t do anything stupid.” They took his ribbing without comment. It was the only comfort he knew how to give. “Those who stand together….”

“Cannot be broken,” they finished.

Varick studied their faces. Trevor was the older of the two but still very much a kid. Enzo looked at the world with a glimmer of hope in his eyes—a spark Varick had watched fade from Trevor’s almost entirely.

The wind howled slow and deep, enough that Harou answered.

“Easy, boy.” Varick stroked the wolf’s head. “Let’s go.”

He made his way past the soldiers’ smoke rings and the smell of burnt skewers. Bertram leaned against an old acacia trunk nearby, steady as the tree itself. Like a man who’d learned to be elsewhere.

Varick wished he could do the same.

Before he disappeared into the dark, he caught a glimpse of Trevor’s face still watching from the doorway. He held it for a moment—the boy’s expression, the question in it.

He turned away and let the dark take him.

Something low left his mouth—words the wind caught before anyone else could. Whether prayer or habit or something older than either, it dissolved into the dark as he walked ahead.

“Forgive me.”

The wind swallowed it whole and carried it somewhere he couldn’t follow.

Like it heard his thoughts.

Like it understood his message.

Like it knew the way.

— the prologue of Sorrowroot, in full
